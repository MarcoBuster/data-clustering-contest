Why We Need Female Moderators at Presidential Debates

And why we need more moderators who are women of color.

15 Nov 2019 by Joanne Bagshaw Ph.D.

Joanne Bagshaw Ph.D.

The Third Wave

White male journalists continue to report most news, especially prime time evening broadcasts. Research conducted by Time’s Up, for instance, found that among 132 primary debates in presidential races from 1996 through 2016, 44% had zero female moderators and only 27% had at least one person of color moderating. The fifth Democratic debate will be broadcast by MSNBC in Georgia on November 20th, and will be co-hosted by journalists Rachel Maddow, Andrea Mitchell, Kristen Welker, and Ashley Parker. The all-women moderators—the majority of whom are white women-- in the upcoming Democratic Debate reflect the upward trend of gender diversity in moderators, and also the lack of racial equity, as Black or Latinx moderators are still in the minority.

Representation matters, and seeing women in positions of leadership, expressing their authority is an important step in challenging patriarchal norms. And it’s also beyond time that we not only hold our politicians accountable for representing the interests of all or their constituents but that we prioritize the election of politicians that are not beholden to a white male bias. Debate moderators hold tremendous power, as they decide which questions to ask, shape the conversation at the debate, and convey what is of interest to voters.

What do women voters want?

Women want equality. According to a comprehensive study that surveyed 2,000 women voters across the United States, conducted by Breakthrough Campaigns along with the Supermajority Education Fund, women across all demographics do not feel they are treated equally in the United States. For instance, women report that the economy is not working for them and has become worse under the Trump Administration. This is especially true for women of color, and for women who were already struggling financially. Women do not feel valued at work. They report being sexually harassed, paid less than their male colleagues, held back from promotions, and having experienced pregnancy discrimination. Women also don’t feel safe. The threat or experience of sexual harassment on the street or other public places, along with the constant threat of sexual assault, are reminders that safety is a privilege for some women but should be a right for all women. Women also report that their bodies are not respected. Their health concerns are not taken as seriously as men’s and women want to have control over their reproduction. Lastly, women want a representative government that reflects the diverse backgrounds, races, and genders of their constituents.

There are no women’s issues

Women are not a “special interest group,” and women’s issues are relevant for everyone. Closing the wage gap and raising the minimum wage helps stabilize a family’s income. Gun control policies that restrict domestic abusers' access to guns help keep everyone safe. Climate change disproportionally affects women's and girl’s health, and also affects families and communities. Most women don’t get pregnant alone. Women’s issues affect everyone, and women voters report that categorizing social issues as relating to women tells men that this is not their concern when in fact they are everyone’s concern.

Currently, the United States Congress is comprised of only 24% of women. In order for women of all demographics to achieve equality, we need more institutional power. That means representation in leadership positions that include the government, media, and law so that we are the ones making the decisions that affect our lives.

What questions should the moderators ask?

The questions that the moderators ask should reflect the concerns and priorities of voters. In the upcoming debate, moderators should ensure at the very least the following questions related to gender equality should be answered by each candidate:

  • What is your plan to ensure that the economy works for everyone, particularly low-income women of color? Will your plan include raising the minimum wage, and provide quality, affordable childcare? Does your plan also include mandatory paid family and medical leave?
  • What solutions do you propose to protect women from sexual harassment?
  • What policies would you implement to close the wage and opportunity gap, particularly for lesbian, bisexual, trans, and women of color?
  • If you are elected president, how will you protect women’s reproductive rights and high rates of maternal mortality, especially for black women?
  • What specifically will you do to address the epidemic of sexual violence against women and girls?
  • Now that the deadline has been lifted, what is your plan to ratify the Equal Rights Amendment?

It's time that the priorities and concerns of women take center stage in the debates, and who's asking the questions and shaping the conversation matters.
